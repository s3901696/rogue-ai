Project Idea: Vaptic Vest

In the vast field of Information Technology, there is a lot to be excited about. However, as a tech geek who is passionate about the future of gaming, I am constantly looking for new innovative technologies that take gaming to the next level. Particularly looking into Virtual and Augmented Reality becoming increasingly popular and standardized as the basis for interactive gameplay. The main inspiration behind the Haptic Vest is from the sci-fi movie Ready Player One, where the player wore a VR Haptic Feedback Suit which would allow them to operate your avatar/character, as well as feel what's happening to their body in the virtual world (also known as the ‘Oasis’).

On the other hand, a Haptic Vest has much more potential than just simply being a piece of high-tech gaming equipment. A haptic vest could also have big applications in areas such as; therapeutics (particularly mental illnesses), emergency services (police, fire, ambulance), as well as military use. One example of the use of a haptic vest in therapeutics would be to give tight hugs (using the haptics of the vest) to relieve stress for people who suffer from anxiety or any other mental illness, which don’t have 24/7 access to a carer. In emergency circumstances, this vest would also be very practical in multiple instances. For medical purposes, the vest could monitor a person’s heart rate, fitness level, and body vitals, monitoring their health and wellbeing. Most importantly in the case where the heart stops beating, the vest could automatically use its haptic technology to conduct CPR without the use of a second person. In addition to this, if CPR isn’t sufficient to save the person, it could automatically use electrical pulses (built-in defibrillator) as a last resort to save their life. A practical military-spec version of the vest could be used in the scenario someone is shot or stabbed in combat, the vest could detect the injury and seal around the wound (using the haptics and flexible polymeric self-repairing material) by applying pressure to the area affected, preventing further bleeding and loss of blood.

To outline, the overall idea of the project is to create a simplified version of a ‘haptic-feedback vest’, with the capabilities of responding to computer inputs. To keep this project on a feasible level, the aim is to represent the vest with a piece of material stretched out over a square frame, with many small metal studs as well as fewer electromagnets spread over it. The electromagnets will be connected with wires to a small box (with Arduino), which communicates (most likely connected to pc) with the computers’ software program. The program, on a graphic level; will be a basic x by y, square graph of the mapped electromagnets, allowing the user to select and trigger (causing the voltage to run through the wires turning the selected electromagnets on) any area of the material to simulate the haptics of a compressing section of a ‘vest’. However, it is also possible to use Blynk to create an app on an iPhone/Android device which can communicate wirelessly to the Arduino to controller the electromagnets.

In order for this project to produce a tangible product, there will be some basic software and hardware skills needed. To begin with, there will have to be some fundamental software skills needed to create a program (using Arduinos’ CC+ ‘language’ or Blynk) that can trigger selected electromagnets at a safe voltage (1.5 - 2 volts in parallel). In the case this may be troublesome, parts of the code may be sourced from existing GitHub repositories. Furthermore, there is quite a lot of hardware involved with the project as well as some skills with electronics. Hardware needed includes; a computer/tablet /phone, many tiny magnetic metal studs, a few electromagnets, Arduino, relay module, I/O pins, wires, some piece of polymer material, and possibly some more electronics. Moreover, putting the hardware together will also require some skills in electric circuits. I believe that this project isn’t beyond what is feasible and with some extended research and development behind a solid team, the simplified concept of a ‘haptic vest’ is possible to engineer. The hardware isn’t expensive or rare and is easy to attain, as well as the software is not difficult to learn and integrate into the hardware after some experimenting with it.

With the overarching goal of creating a basic idea of a simple functioning ‘haptic vest’, there may be some potential drawbacks or obstacles to encounter along the way. With software as well as many hardware components communicating with each other, there are bound to be issues and problems with the limited understanding of how a ‘haptic vest’ should operate. Considering the fact that I could not find a ‘haptic vest’ that functions similarly to the one thought out in this project, is a concerning factor to this ‘innovation’. Drawbacks in the project could be from factors such as; code, electrical connections, voltage distribution, or any other piece of hardware integrated into this ‘haptic vest’ that I may understand.

On the other hand, if the project is successful it could potentially prove or inspire the idea of integrating more advanced electromagnetic technology into vests for gaming, therapeutic, medical, or even military use. By being able to equip the disabled or mentally ill with a vest that could monitor their health state and perform CPR in case of emergency, the carer does not need to worry if they must leave their patient for a short time, as they can stay assured that the vest is keeping them safe and supported. The success of this project could also lead to saving the lives of those who are in the military and could potentially be injured in combat and are in need of instant pressure to a puncture in their upper body area so that they don’t lose blood. Lastly, for gamers who want to be immersed in their VR game to the fullest extent, by wearing an advanced version of the ‘haptic vest’ they could feel the sense of touch, in the virtual world. This could be things like; ‘vibrations’ from an in-game shock wave from an explosion, a ‘tap’ on the shoulder from an in-game character, or the ‘tingle’ of an in-game bug walking on your back. Whichever way the high-tech version of the ‘haptic vest’ is used, it will innovate and move forward the tech industry in more ways than one.
